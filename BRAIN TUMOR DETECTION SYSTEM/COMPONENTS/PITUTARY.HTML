Pituitary Tumors

Understanding Symptoms, Causes, Diagnosis & Treatment

What Are Pituitary Tumors?

Pituitary tumors are abnormal growths that develop in the pituitary gland. Most pituitary tumors are noncancerous (benign) growths that remain in the pituitary gland or surrounding tissues. These tumors are relatively common in adults, and many don't cause symptoms or require treatment.

The pituitary gland is a small, bean-shaped gland situated at the base of your brain, behind the bridge of your nose. Despite its small size (about the size of a pea), the pituitary gland is called the "master gland" because it produces hormones that control many other endocrine glands in your body.

Symptoms & Signs

Symptoms of pituitary tumors vary depending on whether they are functioning (hormone-producing) or nonfunctioning tumors, as well as which hormones they affect:

General Symptoms (Due to Tumor Growth)

  • Headaches - Often severe and persistent
  • Vision problems - Including loss of peripheral vision or double vision
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Confusion
  • Dizziness
  • Loss of consciousness (rare)

Hormone-Related Symptoms

  • Overproduction of growth hormone: Acromegaly (gigantism in children), enlarged hands and feet, coarsened facial features
  • Overproduction of prolactin: Irregular menstrual periods, milk production in women not nursing (galactorrhea), erectile dysfunction in men
  • Overproduction of ACTH: Cushing's syndrome - weight gain, easy bruising, muscle weakness, diabetes, high blood pressure
  • Underproduction of hormones: Fatigue, weakness, sensitivity to cold, decreased sexual interest, infertility

Causes & Risk Factors

The exact cause of pituitary tumors is not well understood. Most pituitary tumors are sporadic - they're not inherited and occur in people with no family history of pituitary problems.

Potential Causes

  • Genetic alterations - Certain gene mutations may cause abnormal cell growth in the pituitary gland
  • Hereditary disorders - Rarely, pituitary tumors can be associated with genetic syndromes such as multiple endocrine neoplasia type 1 (MEN 1)

Risk Factors

  • Family history of pituitary tumors or endocrine disorders
  • Age - Most common in adults, though they can occur at any age
  • Certain hereditary conditions like MEN 1, Carney complex, or familial isolated pituitary adenoma

Diagnosis

Diagnosing pituitary tumors typically involves several tests and procedures:

Physical Examination

Your doctor will look for signs and symptoms that might indicate a pituitary tumor or hormone abnormalities.

Blood and Urine Tests

These tests measure hormone levels to detect abnormal pituitary function, including:

  • Growth hormone levels
  • Prolactin levels
  • ACTH and cortisol levels
  • Thyroid hormone levels
  • Sex hormone levels (estrogen, testosterone)

Imaging Tests

  • MRI - Primary imaging test for pituitary tumors, providing detailed images of the pituitary gland
  • CT scan - May be used if MRI is not possible

Vision Testing

Since pituitary tumors can press on the optic nerves, comprehensive vision testing may be performed to check for vision loss.

Additional Tests

  • Stimulation or suppression tests to evaluate hormone production
  • Growth hormone suppression test

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the tumor size, whether it's functional (hormone-producing), and your overall health. Options include:

Wait and See (Observation)

For small, non-functional tumors that aren't causing symptoms, regular monitoring may be recommended.

Medications

Certain medications can reduce tumor size or normalize hormone production:

  • Dopamine agonists (cabergoline, bromocriptine) for prolactin-secreting tumors
  • Somatostatin analogs for growth hormone-secreting tumors
  • Hormone replacement therapy for underactive pituitary function

Surgery

Transsphenoidal surgery (through the nose and sphenoid sinus) is the most common approach. For larger tumors, a craniotomy may be necessary.

Radiation Therapy

May be used after surgery for residual tumor tissue or when surgery isn't possible:

  • Stereotactic radiosurgery (focused radiation)
  • Conventional radiation therapy
